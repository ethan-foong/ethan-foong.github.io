One of the questions I keep coming back to in my research is deceptively simple: what does it actually mean for a robot to *play*?

In humans, play is easy to recognize but hard to define. A child stacking blocks isn't optimizing for anything in particular — they're exploring what's possible, building intuition about physics, occasionally knocking things over just to see what happens. There's no reward signal. There's no task.

## Why this matters for manipulation

Most robot learning today is deeply task-centric. You define a reward, collect demonstrations, or specify a goal state. The robot learns to hit that target. It works surprisingly well — but it also means the robot is only ever as capable as the tasks you thought to train it on.

The alternative vision is a robot that accumulates a broad repertoire of skills through open-ended interaction, then *composes* those skills when a specific task comes along. Less like a specialist, more like a curious generalist.

The challenge is that "open-ended interaction" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. Without some structure, random exploration in a high-dimensional action space is just noise.

## What makes exploration useful?

A few properties seem important:

- **Novelty**: the robot should prefer states it hasn't visited before, which drives coverage of the state space.
- **Learnability**: not all novel states are equally useful. A state that's hard to reach reproducibly doesn't give you much to build on.
- **Composability**: ideally, the skills discovered can be combined. A grasp learned during play should be usable downstream.

The tricky part is that these objectives can conflict. Maximally novel states are often hard to reach reliably. Highly composable skills might cluster around a narrow region of the state space.

## Where I think this is going

My intuition is that the answer involves something like *curriculum over intrinsic motivation* — starting with simple novelty-seeking behavior, then gradually shifting toward more structured exploration as the robot builds up a model of what it can reliably do.

Whether that's the right framing, I'm not sure yet. But it's the question I keep coming back to.
